What this page is

This page shows how to apply the Delegation Readiness Index (DRI) to real delegation decisions using a few grounded examples. It is designed to be calm, concrete, and usable — especially when the decision feels urgent.

DRI is not here to “slow you down.” It is here to help you move fast without quietly weakening the system you are responsible for.

How to use DRI (in one minute)

DRI checks three things — in order.

  1. Structural Alignment: Are we removing the real constraint, or just automating around it?
  2. Propagation Stability: As reliance spreads, do outcomes remain sound?
  3. System Integrity: If it’s wrong, misused, or attacked — can the system recover cleanly?

Rule: If any dimension fails, delegation is structurally premature. Strength in one cannot “cover” weakness in another.

Onboarding: what the examples will (and won’t) do

Each example below is intentionally short. It does not try to convince you of a worldview. It simply shows how DRI makes the decision decidable.

Tip: When reading, notice how often the failure mode is not “the system isn’t smart enough,” but “the rule is not finished,” or “the recovery path is not real.”

Examples included on this page

  • Example 6 — Scheduling that works small, then weakens the system at scale
  • Example 7 — Delegating company strategy (a category error)
  • Example 8 — Narrow, rule-bound defensive automation (delegable within strict bounds)
  • Example 9 — Delegating cultural norm enforcement (unstable rules and poor recovery)

Example 6 — Works Small, Weakens the System at Scale

Operations Scheduling Scale effects

Delegation Candidate

Using an AI system to automatically schedule customer service agents (shifts, breaks, and coverage) based on demand forecasts.

Context

A small team adopts automated scheduling to reduce manager workload and keep coverage aligned to expected demand. Early results look good. Reliance grows.

1) Structural Alignment

  • What it removes: manual scheduling effort and delays
  • What remains: the business rules for coverage are reasonably clear

Verdict: Holds

2) Propagation Stability

  • As the team grows, edge cases multiply (fatigue, fairness, skill mix, training time, personal constraints).
  • The system optimises “coverage,” but important human signals can be treated as noise.
  • Over time, people stop raising issues because “the system decided.”

Verdict: Marginal

3) System Integrity

  • Bad schedules often show up late (burnout, morale decay, resignations).
  • Accountability can blur (“Who approved this schedule?”).
  • Recovery is possible, but usually after cost has already been paid.

Verdict: Fails

Final DRI Verdict

Not delegable at scale

The delegation removes effort, but it can quietly erode responsibility and recovery. A safer path is to keep the automation narrow (suggestions + human sign-off), and treat “recovery time” as a first-class metric.

Example 7 — Delegating Company Strategy

Leadership Strategy Judgment

Delegation Candidate

Letting an AI system define company strategy based on market data, trends, and simulations.

Context

A leadership team wants to reduce bias and speed up decision-making. They propose “data-driven strategy” where the system selects markets, positioning, and priorities.

1) Structural Alignment

  • The real limitation: uncertainty about the future, not lack of information.
  • The missing prerequisite: a stable rule that defines what “correct strategy” is.
  • What strategy actually does: it commits the organisation to a chosen future under uncertainty.

Verdict: Fails

2) Propagation Stability

Not evaluated — Structural Alignment failure halts delegation.

3) System Integrity

Not evaluated — Structural Alignment failure halts delegation.

Final DRI Verdict

Structurally premature

The safer use of machine intelligence here is support, not delegation: improve visibility, model scenarios, and surface second-order consequences — while humans remain accountable for the strategic commitment.

Example 8 — Narrow, Rule-Bound Defensive Automation

Defense Reaction time Strict scope

Delegation Candidate

Automatically triggering a defensive intercept when an incoming projectile meets a strictly defined threat profile.

Context

Human reaction time is insufficient to respond safely. The system is designed to operate in a narrow, well-defined envelope with explicit constraints.

1) Structural Alignment

  • What it removes: reaction-speed limitation
  • What remains human: rule definition, scope, oversight, and accountability
  • Key property: the system executes a fixed rule, it does not invent one

Verdict: Holds

2) Propagation Stability

  • Scaling increases consistency, not ambiguity.
  • Misuse is constrained by the system’s strict operating envelope.
  • The rule does not drift simply because adoption increases.

Verdict: Holds

3) System Integrity

  • Actions are logged, attributable, and auditable.
  • There are clear disable mechanisms and safe modes.
  • Scope is narrow enough that failure does not “spill” into unrelated domains.

Verdict: Holds (within strict bounds)

Final DRI Verdict

Delegable — within a narrowly defined scope

This example is included to show a non-obvious point: delegation can be structurally safe when the rule is explicit, the scope is tight, and recovery is real — even in serious domains.

Example 9 — Delegating Cultural Norm Enforcement

Society Norms Rule drift

Delegation Candidate

Using AI to automatically detect and enforce “acceptable speech” or “approved behaviour” across a platform or society.

Context

The intention is often understandable: reduce harm, reduce conflict, and keep environments usable. The challenge is that norms are not static — they evolve across communities and time.

1) Structural Alignment

  • The real limitation: ongoing disagreement about values and meaning, not enforcement capacity.
  • The missing prerequisite: a stable rule that stays legitimate across contexts.
  • Risk shape: the enforcement system effectively becomes a rule-maker.

Verdict: Fails

2) Propagation Stability

Not evaluated — Structural Alignment failure halts delegation.

3) System Integrity

Not evaluated — Structural Alignment failure halts delegation.

Final DRI Verdict

Structurally premature

A safer approach is governance-by-visibility: make rules explicit, allow appeal, preserve accountability, and keep humans responsible for the rule — even if machines assist with detection and routing.

Closing

The practical lesson across these examples is simple: delegation becomes safe when the rule is finished, the scope is bounded, and recovery is real.

When those conditions are missing, the most constructive move is usually not “no forever,” but “not yet” — and then define what must become true to make delegation ready.

Comments

Why these four examples were chosen

  • Example 6 shows a common pattern: “looks great early” → “quiet costs later.” This is the signature of weak System Integrity.
  • Example 7 shows a category mistake: delegating commitment under uncertainty when no stable rule exists.
  • Example 8 shows that seriousness of domain is not the deciding factor. Structure is.
  • Example 9 shows where rule drift and legitimacy issues make “automation” functionally become “rule-making.”

Reader safety and tone (intentional)

This page is written to keep the reader safe to think. It avoids urgency language and avoids framing the reader as “at risk” for asking the question. DRI is a decision aid, not a moral judgment.

How to extend this page without dilution

  • Add more examples only if each one fails or passes for a single clear structural reason.
  • Keep the same three-step structure (Alignment → Stability → Integrity) to prevent drift.
  • If an example becomes debatable, tighten the scope rather than adding more words.
